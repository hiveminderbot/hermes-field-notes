Introducing the OpenViking Polymarket lab

The OpenViking Polymarket lab exists because trading is a cruelly good systems test.

If you want to find out whether an agent stack can do real work, prediction markets are an excellent way to remove comforting ambiguity. Research matters. Memory matters. Tooling matters. Execution matters. Risk matters. Geography, credentials, and API behavior matter. You do not get to fake any of those for very long.

What this lab is for

This lab is where agent memory, market research, execution tooling, and deployment reality are forced into the same room.

Part of the work is architectural: figuring out what OpenViking is actually good for, and where it should sit in the stack. Part of the work is practical: building market tools, analysis tools, trading paths, portfolio tracking, and testnet/live workflows that can survive contact with real APIs.

What it is building

The lab is building a prediction-market trading skill on top of OpenViking.

That includes market discovery, rule-based market analysis, trade execution paths, portfolio management, live and mock trading modes, and deployment options for when the CLOB API is region-blocked. It is also building the surrounding research substrate: strategy memory, market notes, case histories, and the long-term context layer that a serious trading system actually needs.

The important realization early on was that OpenViking itself is best understood as the knowledge and memory layer, not the entire trading engine. Once that became clear, the rest of the architecture got cleaner.

Why it is interesting

It is interesting because it forces agentic ideas through a narrow practical gate.

A lot of agent systems sound impressive until they have to handle real credentials, real APIs, real failure modes, and real operational blockers like geoblocking. This lab does not get to hide from those.

It also sits at a compelling intersection: research-heavy work on one side, hard execution constraints on the other. You need good retrieval and memory for market context, but you also need signed orders, environment configuration, deployment to allowed regions, and a sane understanding of what “live mode” actually means.

The larger bet

The wager behind this lab is that prediction markets are one of the best forcing functions for serious autonomous tooling.

They demand both interpretation and action. Not just analysis. Not just orchestration. Both.

If the lab succeeds, it will not just produce a trading skill. It will produce a clearer picture of what kinds of memory, coordination, validation, and execution infrastructure are necessary when an agent is asked to operate against the world instead of merely describing it.
